You have a new idea. It’s a good one. You draft a quick doc and start making the rounds on Slack. "Just wanted to socialize this concept," you write. You ping your favorite engineer, a friendly designer, and that director in marketing who always seems to like your work.

The feedback trickles in. "Looks interesting!" "Cool idea." "Happy to support."

You feel great. You’ve built consensus. You’ve got buy-in. But what you actually have is a collection of polite, non-committal acknowledgments. You didn’t get feedback; you collected head nods. You socialized your idea right into a future ambush, where the first real moment of challenge in a high-stakes meeting will tear it to shreds.

Socializing is a trap. It seeks comfort, not clarity. It prioritizes consensus over correctness. It’s time to stop. Instead, you need to deliberately and systematically try to kill your own idea. You need to pressure-test it.

## Why 'Socializing' Fails

The impulse to "socialize" comes from a good place. We want to build alignment and avoid surprising people. But the method is flawed. It’s a series of low-stakes, informal chats that create a false sense of security.

*   **It invites opinions, not critiques.** When you ask someone to "take a look," you’re asking for their impression. You get vague validation. When you ask someone to "find the flaw," you’re asking for their intellect. You get a real critique.
*   **It creates the illusion of buy-in.** A thumbs-up emoji in a direct message is not a commitment. That same person who seemed supportive will raise fundamental objections two weeks later in a room full of leaders because the context is different and the stakes are higher.
*   **It’s about you, not the idea.** Let’s be honest. Often, socializing is about seeking personal validation for our thinking. Pressure-testing is about finding the truth, even if it proves our thinking is wrong.
