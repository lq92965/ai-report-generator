The demo is always flawless. In a slick, pre-recorded video, a new enterprise AI assistant digests a decade of sales data, identifies three underserved markets, and drafts a complete go-to-market strategy, complete with ad copy. The CEO on stage promises a new era of corporate agility. The stock gets a little bump.

Back in the real world, that same company’s marketing team just spent six weeks arguing with the finance department to get API access to the quarterly expense reports.

This is the disconnect no one at the keynotes wants to talk about. The biggest barrier to artificial intelligence is not the technology. It’s the org chart. The most sophisticated large language model on the planet is about to run headfirst into the unmovable wall of a mid-level manager’s political fiefdom.

For decades, large corporations have been built on a simple principle: information is power. The department with the proprietary data—the customer list, the supply chain logistics, the performance metrics—holds the cards. Their processes are their own, their software is customized, and their budget is defended with the ferocity of a territorial animal. This structure was designed for stability, not speed. It functions like a corporate immune system, efficiently identifying and neutralizing foreign agents.

Today, the foreign agent is the AI.

These new systems are worthless if they are starved of information. Their entire purpose is to see across the silos that define the modern company. An AI that can analyze marketing spend against sales outcomes and supply chain costs in real time is a miracle of efficiency. It is also a direct threat to the three different Vice Presidents who control that information. Giving an AI access to everything is a political act of surrender. It requires a level of trust and transparency that most corporate cultures actively punish.

So we get stuck in pilot program purgatory. A small, sanitized dataset is handed over. The AI performs a clever but inconsequential parlor trick. A press release is issued. Nothing of substance actually changes, because changing would mean disrupting the delicate balance of power that keeps the enterprise running. The problem isn't that the AI can't integrate with the legacy Oracle database from 1998. The real problem is that the person who administers that database sees the AI as an existential threat to their department’s relevance
