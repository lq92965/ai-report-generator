You stare at the Jira ticket. The user story, generated by an AI in 12 seconds, is perfect. It’s grammatically correct. It follows the "As a [user], I want [feature], so that [benefit]" template flawlessly. The acceptance criteria are logical, tidy bullet points.

So why does it feel so... empty?

You copied it. You pasted it. You assigned it. But you didn't *feel* it. And if you can't feel the user's problem, neither can the engineer who's about to build the solution. You’ve just outsourced the most human part of your job: empathy.

## The Hallucination of a Perfect Requirement

We ask AI to write a user story, and it gives us a plausible-sounding artifact. It looks like a requirement. It smells like a requirement. But it’s a clever synthesis of every other user story it has ever ingested, devoid of your specific user’s context, frustration, and unique worldview.

An AI doesn't know about the support ticket from Sarah in accounting, who almost threw her monitor out the window last Tuesday. It hasn't sat through the user interview where Mark sighed heavily and said, "I just wish it would do this one thing." It hasn't seen the Slack thread where the sales team lost a deal because of this exact missing feature.

The AI gives you the what. It completely misses the why. And the *why* is what inspires an engineer to find a clever solution instead of just checking the boxes.

## AI Can't Fake Empathy

The goal of a user story isn't just to define scope. It's to transmit a feeling. It’s a tiny vessel of narrative that carries the user’s pain from a research call all the way to a line of code. When you let an AI write the core of that story, you break the chain.

You get a technically sound instruction, but you lose the soul. The result is a feature that works, but doesn't delight. It's a house built perfectly to code, with no one living inside.

## A Better Way: Use AI as Your Research Assistant, Not Your Writer

Stop asking the AI to write for you. Start asking it to think *with* you. Treat it like a brilliant, lightning-fast junior PM who can sift through mountains of data but needs your guidance to find the meaning.

### Step 1: Feed It the Raw Ingredients

Don't give the AI a vague prompt like, "Write a user story for a new dashboard filter." Instead, feed it the real stuff. Paste in the raw transcript from a user interview. Give it a dozen angry customer support tickets about the same issue. Feed it the Loom video where the user struggles to find what they need.

Then, ask questions:
*   "Based on these 10 support tickets, what are the three most common words these users are using to describe their frustration?"
*   "Summarize the key pain point from this interview transcript in one sentence."
*   "Identify potential edge cases based on this user's failed workflow."

### Step 2: Ask for Structure, Not Soul

Once you have the raw insights, use the AI for scaffolding. It’s fantastic at creating structure around a core idea that you provide. The soul comes from you; the skeleton can come from the machine.

You write the core story: "As Sarah in accounting, I need to find an invoice from last quarter without clicking 12 times, so I don't miss our payment deadline and feel like a failure."

Then you ask the AI:
*   "Draft five acceptance criteria for this story."
*   "List the potential technical dependencies for this feature."
*   "Rephrase this story for a stakeholder update email."

### Step 3: You Are the Final Human Filter

The AI's output is your first draft, not your final ship. Your job is to take the structured output and breathe life back into it. Read the story and the acceptance criteria aloud. Does it sound like Sarah? Does it capture the urgency from that sales call?

Cross out the robotic phrases. Add a quote from the user directly into the ticket description. Link to the Loom video. Your final touch is what transforms a sterile instruction into a compelling mission for your team.

## The Story Is Yours to Tell

An AI can assemble a user story. But it can’t tell one. It can’t connect an engineer’s work to a real person’s struggle. That's your job. By using AI as a tool for synthesis rather than a substitute for thought, you keep yourself close to the user. You build your own empathy muscle. And you ship products that have a pulse.
