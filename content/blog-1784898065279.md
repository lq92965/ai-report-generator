The room is quiet except for the sound of your clicks. You’re walking the executive team through the new dashboard. "And here," you say, pointing your cursor, "is the new date-range filter. You can select a start date and an end date, and the chart will update." You click a few times to show it works. They nod. A few are checking their phones under the table.

You just spent six weeks of your team's life building this. You nailed the sprint goals. But you can feel the energy draining from the room. You’re showing them a collection of parts, not a solution.

You’ve fallen into the Feature Tour trap. You’re narrating the UI instead of telling the story of the value you created.

## The Anatomy of a Bad Demo

The Feature Tour happens because you’re too close to the work. You’re proud of the clever filter, the snappy API response, the clean interface. So you show it off, piece by piece.

*   "First, the user logs in."
*   "Then, they navigate to this screen."
*   "We added a new button here."
*   "When you click it, this modal appears."

This is a chronological report of user actions. It forces your audience to do the hard work of connecting those actions to a meaningful outcome. They have to guess *why* this matters. Most of them won't bother. They'll just ask tactical questions like, "Can it export to PDF?" and you'll get dragged into a conversation about future features before they even understand the current one.

## Reframe: From Click-Path to Conflict-Resolution

A great demo isn't a tour of a house, showing off the plumbing and the light fixtures. It's a story about a family who was living in a cramped apartment and now has a place to grow.

To stop giving feature tours, use a simple story structure. Every good story has a character, a conflict, and a resolution.

**1. The Character and Their Problem:** Start with the user. Give them a name. Anchor the demo in a real, painful problem that a real person was having. Use a direct quote if you have one.

*   **Instead of:** "We built a new reporting dashboard."
*   **Try:** "I was talking to Sarah, our head of marketing. She told me she has to pull numbers from three different systems every Monday morning to see if last week's campaigns worked. It takes her two hours and she still doesn't trust the data."

**2. The Old, Awful Way:** Before you show your beautiful new solution, remind everyone of the pain. Describe the workaround. Make the audience feel the frustration. This creates tension.

*   **Instead of:** "Here's the new interface."
*   **Try:** "This is the spreadsheet Sarah used to build. She’d export a CSV from Salesforce, another from Google Analytics, and then manually VLOOKUP everything together. One wrong copy-paste and the whole thing was off."
